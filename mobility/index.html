The baseline of 0 was set from data collected over 5 weeks, between January 3rd and February 6th, 2020. On Tuesday February 18th, folks were still more or less going about their normal routines.

Then lockdown took effect, each week fewer and fewer people went into work, peaking on March 24th, 2020. Here a pattern emerges—people in largely rural Trump counties (based on 2016 presidential vote-share) continued to go into workplaces more than people in more urban, Democratic counties.

Let’s track the return to work for two counties, one at each extreme. Cook county, Illinois is home to Chicago and the surrounding suburbs. Two hundred miles to the south lies Shelby county, where TK% work in agrigulture or manufacturing.

Over 2020 and into 2021, people in both counties steadily returned to workplaces. While there were consistently more people at work in Shelby County, workers in Cook county returned at around the same rate.

And in fact, if we plot the change in the index between March 24th, 2020 and May 11th, 2021…

…we see the correlation to vote-share dissapear.

In general, Democratic and Republican counties are returning to work at the similar rates. While overall, these data suggest that people living in Republican, working-class counties spent more time in their workplaces during the pandemic, there is no indication that Republican politics or policies have caused workers to return to work faster, as restrictions have relaxed.
